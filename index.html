How do we use visual representations to

Visual media pervade our lives. Follow that arrow to get here. Swipe on this. Close ad there. In fact, our ability to produce and understand symbolic communication is a defining characteristic of what it means to be human—predating even written language by thousands of years and spanning most cultures.

I explore questions like: How are we able to learn and convey so much meaning through visual form? How does this ability develop over childhood? My work aims to answer these questions by leveraging modern crowdsourcing techniques and computer vision to systematically characterize the semantic content of visual representations.

I'm a 4th-year PhD student in Experimental Psychology. I work with Dr. Judith Fan in the Cognitive Tools Lab at Stanford (2023) and Dr. Caren Walker in the Early Learning & Cognition Lab at the University of California San Diego.

Before graduate school, I studied Philosophy and the History of Mathematics & Sciences at St. John's College where I became fascinated by the coicidence of major scientific advancements and the development of novel visualizations (e.g., Cartesian coordinate system, periodic table, Vitruvian man).

research

I am broadly interested in questions of visualization, design, and social communication. Below are three lines of work exploring adaptive visualization strategies across different domains:

What drives recognizability of different visual object concepts?

From children's drawings of common objects to adults' abstractions of categories and subcategories, I study how the semantic structure of our visualizations change across childhood and flexibly depending on our visual context. By analyzing large-scale datasets of 2K children's drawings to 20K adult drawings, my work aims to comprehensively explore how people choose to represent different visual object concepts. Our datasets will be publically released shortly!

How do explanatory visualizations convey higher-level knowledge?

Visual explanations are at the core of human innovation, collaboration, and communication. I study how people encode their knowledge about physical mechanisms into sketches. I then crowdsource semantic annotations through custom web browser experiments and use computer vision techniques to analyze the drawn content and its spatial organization to determine how people prioritize different kinds of visual information depending on their viewers' goals.

How do data visualization conventions guide interpretations?

Graphs are poweful tools to compress large data, reveal latent trends, and communicate vital knowledge to a wide audience. However, data visualizations are far from objective. For example, how might a scientist vs. a journalist flexible generate such visualizations? I study how people produce graphs in order to convey different aspects of statistical patterns.

Beyond research, I am also passionate about teaching computational literacy and research design skills to undergraduate researchers, as well as thinking about strategies to increase healthier mentorship relationships in academia.
